Introduction and Purpose

This document motivates and specifies the design of the Open Science Slideshow Reusable Learning Object Project (the slideshow) as required in Richard Huntrod's Computer Science 601 course -- Survey of Computing and Information Systems -- at Athabasca University in Winter 2016.

The purpose of the slideshow is to provide learners with recommendations and best practices for publishing the results of scientific research in an open manner.

Description of Target Audience

The slideshow is intended for post-secondary learners with an interest in performing and publishing scientific research. Some basic computing and science knowledge is required.

It is intended for English language learners only.

For maximum reach, the slideshow is available on the web as a Reveal.js HTML5 presentation, free of charge, under the MIT License. It is also mobile accessible.

Slideshow Objectives

By the end of the slideshow, learners should be able to:

  • Explain the motivations behind Open Science.
  • Enumerate the elements of an Open Science project.
  • For each element:
    • Define the scope of the element.
    • Outline example implementations of the element.

Slideshow Design Outline

What is Open Science?

  • Provide definitions of both "Open" and "Science"
  • Integrate these definitions

Why Open Science?

  • Motivate Open Science
  • Give examples where Open Science could have helped prevent failures

Elements of Open Science

Kraker et. al. provides a core set of four elements that together comprise an Open Science project:

  • Open Methodology
  • Open Source
  • Open Data
  • Open Access

OpenScienceASAP supplements these with two additional elements:

  • Open Peer Review
  • Open Educational Resources

I propose a seventh element as a way to publically verify claims of scientific priority:

  • Open Priority

The slideshow considers these to be the core elements of Open Science. The slideshow defines and exemplifies each element.

Open Methodology

  • Use non-proprietary, accessible methods, and document them well
  • Example: Patents and Open Methodology

Open Source

  • Use freely accessible, permissively licensed software
  • Example: Workflow for easily reproducible paper

Open Data

  • Use trusted, public data in open formats, and publish novel data similarly.
  • Examples: Various data formats -- pros/cons

Open Access

  • Make research results freely available
  • Example: Compare/contrast open access journals/models

Open Peer Review

  • Facilitate open criticism of work
  • Example: F1000Research

Open Educational Resources

  • Distribute results as freely available, permissively licensed learning objects
  • Example: This slideshow!

Open Priority

  • Use public blockchains over trusted third parties to verify the chronology of research activities.
  • Example: Data stored in the Bitcoin blockchain

Conclusion/Quiz

The slideshow concludes with a short summary of the material covered. A multiple choice quiz is offered to learners to test their retention of the material.
